## Plain-language note

This is a workbench room, not a manifesto. It exists because a move to a republic touches far more than the Crown: it reaches into thousands of treaties Australia has entered over more than a century, most of them signed by the executive without a public vote.

Instead of arguing treaty by treaty, the room offers one shared lens and one shared map, so self-governing people can read any inherited agreement calmly and decide, on purpose, whether to keep it, renew it, modernise it, sunset it on a timeline, or replace it with something freely chosen.

Nothing here declares an outcome. The five questions (who it touches, who it serves, whose consent it rests on, whether it is still alive, and whether to keep or change it) are meant to be asked, not answered in advance. The map groups roughly 2,900 treaties into nine clusters so a person can find a thread to pull, then walk it through the Law Engine, the Truth Engine, the Sovereignty stack, the Cyber-Republic room and the Civic ledger.

The peace-first posture is deliberate. Alliance and security instruments belong on the map because an honest inventory cannot skip them, but they are read the same calm way as a tax treaty or a wetlands convention, and never tied to the drum of any current conflict.
